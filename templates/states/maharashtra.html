Overview

Maharashtra, a state in western India, has diverse agro-climatic conditions, allowing for the cultivation of a variety of crops. The state is a major contributor to India's agricultural output.

Sugarcane

Sugarcane is a major cash crop grown in Maharashtra, contributing significantly to the state's economy. It is cultivated mainly in the western and northern regions, including Kolhapur, Sangli, Satara, Pune, Solapur, and Ahmednagar. Sugarcane is a perennial crop with a cycle of 10 to 18 months, grown under both rainfed and irrigated conditions. Maharashtra has a well-established sugar industry, with many cooperative sugar mills. The crop is used for sugar and ethanol production, and the sector provides significant rural employment.

Jowar (Sorghum)

Jowar is cultivated across Maharashtra, both in rainfed and irrigated areas. It is a Kharif crop, sown with the onset of the monsoon (June–July) and harvested from September to November. Jowar is a staple food in many parts of the state and is also used as fodder. It is valued for its nutritional content and is often included in crop rotation to improve soil fertility.

Rice

Rice is grown in various agro-climatic zones of Maharashtra, in both Kharif and Rabi seasons. Kharif rice is sown with the monsoon (June), especially in coastal and eastern regions, while Rabi rice is grown where irrigation is available. The state cultivates both traditional and high-yielding varieties. Rice is a staple food for a significant portion of the population.

Tur (Pigeon Pea)

Tur is widely grown in Maharashtra, both in rainfed and irrigated areas, mainly as a Kharif crop. It is sown with the onset of the monsoon and harvested from September to November. Tur is an important pulse crop, consumed as dal and used as fodder. It is valued for its protein content and is often included in crop rotation for soil fertility.

Cotton

Cotton is cultivated in Vidarbha, Marathwada, and parts of Western Maharashtra. It is a Kharif crop, sown in June–July and harvested from October to December. Both hybrid and Bt cotton varieties are grown. Cotton is a key cash crop, supporting the textile industry and providing income for farmers. The crop is mainly rainfed, but irrigation is used where available.

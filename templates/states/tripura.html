Agricultural Profile

Climate

Warm and humid subtropical climate with distinct wet and dry seasons. High humidity levels throughout the year support diverse crop cultivation.

Rainfall Pattern

Average annual precipitation of 2200mm, with monsoon season from May to September providing majority of rainfall for agriculture.

Soil Diversity

Five major soil types: Reddish yellow brown sandy soil, Red loam and sandy loam soil, Alluvial soil, Younger alluvial soil, and Lateritic soil.

Cropping Seasons

Aush (1st Kharif): April-June
Aman (2nd Kharif): July-September
Boro (Rabi): September-March

Agricultural Overview

Tripura's agriculture is characterized by its unique combination of traditional rice cultivation, plantation crops like rubber and tea, and emerging horticultural practices. The state's agricultural system is adapted to its hilly terrain, abundant rainfall, and subtropical climate, supporting both subsistence and commercial farming activities.

Plantation Agriculture

Rubber and tea plantations providing sustainable income and employment

Traditional Systems

Indigenous rice varieties, bamboo drip irrigation, and terraced cultivation

Crop Diversification

Expansion into spices, fruits, and cash crops for economic development

Major Crops of Tripura

Rice

Staple Crop
Cultivation Seasons: Three distinct seasons - Aush, Aman, and Boro with different varieties
Varieties Grown: Traditional varieties (Tulaipanji, Gobindobhog) and high-yielding (Swarna, IR varieties)
Growing Conditions: Adequate monsoon rainfall and fertile soils supporting rice cultivation
Cultivation Methods: Both transplanting and direct seeding with wetland cultivation predominant
Irrigation Systems: Rainfed cultivation supplemented by tube wells and traditional bamboo drip irrigation
Government Support: Subsidies for seeds, fertilizers, machinery, and MSP procurement systems

Rubber

Plantation Crop
Growing Regions: Major plantations in West Tripura, South Tripura, and Dhalai districts
Varieties Cultivated: Hevea brasiliensis (rubber tree) with various clones and hybrid varieties
Cultivation Practices: Land preparation, planting, regular tapping, fertilization, and pest management
Tapping Process: Latex collection by tapping tree trunk, typically every alternate day
Processing Methods: Latex processed into sheets, blocks, and concentrate for commercial sale
Economic Impact: Significant rural employment and support for ancillary rubber industries

Tea

Premium Crop
Growing Regions: North Tripura and Dhalai districts with suitable climatic conditions
Tea Varieties: Orthodox and CTC (Crush, Tear, Curl) teas including green, black, and white teas
Cultivation Practices: Land preparation, planting, regular pruning, plucking, and processing
Processing Methods: Withering, rolling, oxidation, drying, and sorting to produce different tea types
Employment Generation: Tea gardens providing livelihoods for thousands of rural families
Market Presence: Tripura tea sold domestically and exported, known for unique flavor and quality

Government Support & Initiatives

Plantation Development

Subsidies for rubber and tea plantation development, including planting material and land preparation support.

Technical Training

Skill development programs for farmers in modern cultivation techniques, processing, and value addition.

Infrastructure Development

Development of rural roads, storage facilities, and processing units to support agricultural marketing.

Welfare Programs

Comprehensive welfare schemes for plantation workers including healthcare, education, and housing facilities.

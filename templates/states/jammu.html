Overview

Jammu and Kashmir's agriculture is shaped by its diverse geography and climate, supporting a variety of crops from the subtropical plains to the temperate valleys. The region is renowned for its high-value crops such as saffron and apples, as well as staple cereals and pulses.

Saffron

Pampore as the Saffron Belt: Pampore, in Pulwama district, is known as the saffron bowl of India due to its ideal climate and soil.
Variety: "Kashmiri Saffron" or "Lachha Saffron" (Crocus sativus) is prized for its flavor, fragrance, and long threads.
Climatic Requirements: Cold winters and dry summers are ideal; flowers bloom in autumn.
Traditional Farming: Saffron is grown using traditional methods in carefully maintained orchards.
Hand Harvesting: Flowers are hand-picked; each yields three red stigmas (saffron threads).
Irrigation: Relies on natural water sources.
Economic Importance: Saffron is a major source of income and has GI (Geographical Indication) status.

Rice

Varieties: Both basmati and non-basmati rice are grown; basmati is especially famous.
Geographical Diversity: Cultivated in plains, valleys, and some hilly areas.
Climatic Conditions: Subtropical to temperate; irrigation from rivers and lakes (e.g., Jhelum).
Irrigation: Traditional and modern methods are used.
Kharif Crop: Sown in monsoon (June–August), harvested in autumn (September–November).
Cultural & Economic Importance: Staple food and key to the region's agricultural economy.

Barley

Altitude and Climate: Grown from plains to high altitudes.
Rabi Crop: Sown in winter (October–December), harvested in spring (April–June).
Soil: Prefers well-drained loamy or sandy soils.
Water Management: Drought-tolerant but benefits from irrigation.
Uses: Food, fodder, and malt for brewing.
Livestock Feed: Straw and grain are important for animals.
Crop Rotation: Helps break disease cycles and improve soil fertility.

Wheat

Climatic Conditions: Rabi (winter) crop, suited to cold winters.
Rabi Crop Cultivation: Sown late October–December, harvested March–May.
Altitude Variation: Grown from Jammu plains to higher elevations.
Irrigation: Rivers and canals support wheat fields.
Soil: Thrives in well-drained loamy soils.
Food Security: Staple food and vital for local diets.
Economic Importance: Consumed locally and traded.
Crop Rotation: Maintains soil health and reduces pests.

Pulses

Varieties: Lentils (masoor), chickpeas (chana), green gram (moong), black gram (urad), kidney beans (rajma), and more.
Climatic Conditions: Mostly grown as Rabi crops in cool, temperate winters.
Crop Rotation: Improve soil fertility by fixing nitrogen.
Food Security: Important protein source and used in traditional dishes.
Irrigation: Rivers and canals support pulse crops.
Traditional Practices: Mix of traditional and modern techniques.
Economic Importance: Consumed locally and sold in markets.

Crops in Punjab

Production Trends (2010–Present)

Area under Cultivation:

4.23 million hectares

Climate:

Continental climate that is semiarid to subhumid.

Rainfall:

273 to 676 mm

Soil Types:

  • Alluvial Soil
  • Sandy Soil
  • Clay Soil
  • Peat Soil
  • Silt Soil
  • Loam Soil

Agricultural Seasons:

  • Kharif/Harrhi (April–September)
  • Rabi/Sauni (October–March)

Wheat

Prominence: Punjab is one of the leading wheat-producing states in India, often called the "Wheat Bowl of India" due to its high productivity and contribution to national wheat production.
Rabi Crop: Wheat is predominantly grown as a Rabi crop (November–April), benefiting from cooler temperatures and adequate moisture.
Varieties: Popular varieties include HD-2967, PBW-343, HD-3086, and HD-2851.
Cultivation Practices: Land preparation, sowing, irrigation, fertilization, and pest management are standard. Zero tillage is gaining popularity.
Irrigation: Extensive canal and tube well irrigation support high yields.
Crop Rotation: Wheat is often rotated with rice, maize, and pulses.
Government Support: Subsidies, credit, crop insurance, and MSP procurement.
Challenges: Declining groundwater, soil degradation, pests, and climate change.

Rice

Area: Rice cultivation is significant in Punjab, especially in districts bordering Haryana and Himachal Pradesh.
Kharif Crop: Sown with the monsoon (June–July), harvested September–October.
Varieties: Both traditional and high-yielding types, with Basmati being especially popular.
Irrigation: Relies heavily on canal and tube well irrigation.
Cultivation: Land preparation, transplanting/direct sowing, irrigation, fertilization, and pest management.
Government Support: Subsidies, credit, crop insurance, and MSP.
Challenges: Groundwater decline, soil degradation, pests, and environmental concerns.

Maize

Area: Grown mainly where irrigation is available.
Kharif Crop: Sown June–July, harvested September–October.
Varieties: Hybrids and improved types.
Cultivation: Land preparation, sowing, irrigation, fertilization, and pest management.
Uses: Primarily for animal feed, poultry, and industry; also consumed as food.
Government Support: Subsidies, extension, and market linkages.
Challenges: Water scarcity, pests, soil degradation, and price fluctuations.

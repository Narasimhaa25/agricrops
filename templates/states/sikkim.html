Cardamom

Growing Regions: Primarily cultivated in the hilly regions of Sikkim, especially at altitudes from 800 to 1500 meters.
Agro-climatic Conditions: Temperate climate, cool temperatures, high humidity, and significant rainfall. Thrives in shaded, forested areas with well-drained soil.
Varieties: Small cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum) and large cardamom (Amomum subulatum, "black cardamom").
Cultivation Practices: Land preparation, planting, shade management, irrigation, fertilization, and pest management. Grown under the canopy of tall trees, propagated by seeds or rhizomes.
Harvesting and Processing: Plants bear fruit after 2–3 years. Pods are harvested green, dried, graded, and packed.
Economic Importance: High-value spice, major income source, known for superior quality and aroma.
Export: Exported to other Indian states and internationally (Nepal, Bhutan, Myanmar).

Ginger and Turmeric

Growing Regions: Hilly regions at 800–1500 meters altitude.
Agro-climatic Conditions: Temperate climate, mild summers, cool winters, ample rainfall, and natural drainage.
Cultivation Practices: Land preparation, planting rhizomes, irrigation, fertilization, weed and pest management, mulching.
Harvesting and Processing: Harvested after 7–9 months, rhizomes are cleaned and dried.
Economic Importance: High-value spices, important for local income and employment.
Organic Cultivation: Sikkim is known for organic farming, adding value to ginger and turmeric.
Market and Export: Sold locally and exported; government supports market linkages and infrastructure.

Rice

Varieties: Both traditional and modern varieties, adapted to local conditions.
Climatic Conditions: Subtropical to alpine climate, adequate rainfall, moderate temperatures.
Terraced Farming: Common due to hilly terrain, helps prevent erosion.
Organic Farming: Sikkim is India's first fully organic state; rice is grown organically.
Local Consumption and Economy: Staple food, significant for local livelihoods.
Challenges: Limited arable land, labor shortages, climate change impacts.

Maize

Varieties: Traditional and hybrid varieties, chosen for climate and market demand.
Climatic Conditions: Subtropical to alpine, adequate rainfall and suitable temperatures.
Cropping Systems: Mono-cropping and intercropping with beans, vegetables, or cardamom.
Terraced Farming: Used to prevent erosion and retain moisture.
Uses: Staple food, fodder, and industrial uses.
Livelihoods: Important for rural communities.
Challenges: Pest/disease management, soil erosion, and market access.
